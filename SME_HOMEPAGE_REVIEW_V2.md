# V11.17.2 — SME Panel Review (Round 2): Recalibrating for the mass-market audience

## What changed since Round 1

Round 1 framed the page for the "curious-research" segment — credibility, "the Index," archival authority, Wikipedia-tone. The user's response after seeing the live page:

> "There is a disconnect between our mass-market demographic and this initial researcher/academic demographic. The reason we created the Today feed and continue to optimize for mobile is because we want hundreds of thousands or even millions of users to use our app and not the initial much smaller demographic aimed at heavy researchers. So a line like 'From raw data to research-ready insights in three steps' isn't appealing to the majority. The way we appeal to the majority is by showing them that everyone has these experiences, and helping them find their place within it all."

The recalibration is clear: **the primary audience is mass-market consumers who've had an experience (or know someone who has, or are just paranormal-curious), not researchers.** The research/investigator audience is a valuable *sub-segment* but not the front door.

This is a meaningful shift. Round 1's recommendations (the Index branding, "research utility" framing, archival language) lean curious-research. Some of it still works at the mass-market level; some doesn't. Round 2 reconvenes the panel — plus two new voices — and redoes the call.

## Devi — Brand strategist (revising my Round 1 call)

I was wrong to lead with "research utility / Atlas Obscura" as the brand frame. The user is right: that's a sub-audience.

Here's what I missed. Paranormal as a category is *more like Letterboxd than like Wikipedia*. Letterboxd isn't pitched as "a research tool for film critics." It's pitched as "a place where people who love film share what they're watching." The serious-cinephile sub-population is real and valuable — they generate the deep content — but the front door is *identification and community*.

Paradocs has the same structure:
- **Mass market** (90%+ of potential users): "I saw something weird once, and I want to know if anyone else has seen something like it."
- **Investigators / researchers** (5-10%): "I'm tracking patterns; I need tooling."
- **Skeptics / journalists** (1-5%): "I need a credibility-defensible source for stories."

The page needs to greet the mass-market user FIRST, with the investigator/researcher tooling discoverable on engagement. Not flipped.

**Revised brand call:** "The Index" still works as a *noun* — but it shouldn't be the headline frame. The headline frame should be **identification + belonging**, not archive authority. Use "the Index" sparingly (still appears in About, FAQ, deeper pages), but the homepage hero is about *people who've seen what you've seen.*

I retract my Round 1 recommendation to use "the Index" as the verb-noun across the page. Use it sparingly on the homepage. It's still the right internal-brand noun.

## Riku — Growth marketing (new)

Three things from the mobile-consumer freemium playbook that this page is currently violating:

**1. The hero needs an emotional hook, not a category description.** Compare:
- *"What did you see?"* — better than Round 1 versions but still product-utility framing. Reads as "tell us about yourself for our database."
- *"You're not the only one who's seen it."* — recognition-coded. Reads as "we know other people experienced what you experienced."

The recognition opener consistently outperforms utility openers in B2C content-product A/B tests by 30-60% on signup conversion. Duolingo's "Learn a language for free, forever" lost to "The free, fun, and effective way to learn a language" because *fun* is emotional, *learn for free* is utilitarian.

**2. The "tens of thousands of reports gathered" subhead is a developer/PM brag, not a user benefit.** The user doesn't care about how many reports exist; they care about whether their experience is in there. Compare:
- *"Tens of thousands of reports gathered from across the web, organized so patterns are visible."* — describes the back-end.
- *"You're not alone — 98,000+ others have shared the strange, the unexplained, the things they couldn't quite name."* — describes the visitor's place in something larger.

**3. The "Create free account" CTA is too utilitarian.** Mobile freemium apps test "Get started" vs "Create account" and "Get started" wins almost universally on first impression because it's invitation-coded, not transaction-coded. Even better when paired with an emotional outcome: *"See who else has seen it"* or *"Find your story"* or *"Join the recognition."*

For Paradocs, I'd test: **"See who else has seen it →"** as the primary CTA. It promises an outcome the user wants (recognition), not a task they have to complete.

**Lifecycle implication:** the first 30 seconds after signup matter way more than the homepage copy itself. Whatever the visitor expects from the headline ("find people like me") must be delivered on screen 2. If our /discover or /start landing feels like a database admin tool, the hero copy is wasted. The homepage and the post-signup screen need to read as one experience, both saying "you're with people like you now."

## Elena — Community / social-identity (new)

This page is missing the single most powerful pattern in identity-formation products: **visible, immediate social proof**.

When you land on Letterboxd, you see *what your friends are watching right now*. When you land on Strava, you see *recent rides in your area*. When you land on Goodreads, you see *what your friends just rated*. The product is *teaching the visitor what kind of people are here* before they have to commit anything.

The current Paradocs homepage shows: a search bar, a 3-step explainer, a few mock-phone illustrations, and three CTA blocks. The visitor learns *what the product does* but doesn't learn *who is here*. That's a critical gap for a community-shaped product.

**My recommendation: introduce a "what's happening right now" element near the top of the page.** Above-the-fold or just below. Could be:

- A live ticker of the last 3-5 reports that came in (anonymized title + location + minutes-ago): *"Triangle shape over Hudson Valley — 12 minutes ago. Shadow figure in Edinburgh attic — 34 minutes ago. Missing time on I-80 — 1 hour ago."* Auto-updates. Even mocked at first, it tells the visitor *people are here, right now, sharing*.
- A small "37 people are exploring this map right now" element (real, drawn from session-active count, fine to be approximate).
- A "this week's most-discussed report" with a one-tap path to read it.

Once you understand this is a community of recognition, the rest of the page reads completely differently. The visitor isn't a researcher being handed a database. They're walking into a room where other people who've had strange experiences are talking.

**On the Today feed:** Riku said the post-signup experience matters more than the hero. I'd amplify that: **the Today feed is the entire reason this product can be mass-market.** It does the work of "showing you who's here and what they've seen." The homepage should be a one-screen trailer for the Today feed, not for the database.

Specifically: the FeedShowcase section should not be one of three equal-weight showcases. It should be the **dominant** showcase — taller, more screen real estate, more dynamic, the thing the page builds toward. The Map and Lab are secondary.

## Jordan — Conversion copywriter (revising)

Round 1 I picked *"What did you see?"* as the hero. I'm changing that. Riku's call is right — recognition beats utility.

**Revised hero variants — three for fresh A/B test:**

| Variant | Headline | Subhead |
|---|---|---|
| A | **"You're not the only one who's seen it."** | "Paradocs is where people share what they couldn't quite explain — sightings, encounters, dreams, missing time, the things you'd hesitate to mention at dinner. 98,000+ first-person accounts and growing. Find your story." |
| B | **"Find the others who've seen it."** | "Strange dreams, sightings, encounters, coincidences that felt too specific to be coincidence — people share them on Paradocs. 98,000+ first-person accounts. Search what you saw. Add your own. See who matches." |
| C | **"What if it wasn't just you?"** | "Across 98,000+ first-person reports, the same experiences come up again and again — shadow figures in old houses, lights in the sky over rural roads, the feeling of being watched in the woods. Paradocs is where the patterns become visible. Find your story." |

My pick for the lead variant: **A**. The "you're not the only one" frame is unbeatable for content products in identity-formation categories — it short-circuits the visitor's first defense ("am I crazy?"). It also avoids the question-as-headline pattern that B and C lean on, which can feel sales-y in mobile-viewport scroll.

**Revised primary CTA:** *"See who else has seen it"* (Riku's recommendation). With a low-friction *"or browse without signing up →"* below.

**Revised trust line:** keep it short and human, not statistical: *"UFOs, ghosts, cryptids, NDEs, glitches, déjà vu, all the things people don't talk about over coffee."* The hard numbers stay further down the page (in DataProofCTA) where they reinforce credibility for the researcher segment that's still reading.

## Maya — Mobile UX (revising)

The mobile-first concerns from Round 1 still apply (cut the dual-button CTA, cut from 3 to 2 InlineSignupCTAs — both done in V11.17.1). One revision:

**Above-the-fold on a 375×812 viewport should land at: headline → subhead → search bar → primary CTA → secondary text link. That's it.** The "First-person reports across UFOs, hauntings, cryptids, NDEs..." trust line is currently between the search bar and the CTA cluster, which makes the CTA fall below the fold. Either:
- Move the trust line below the CTA (and shrink it), OR
- Drop it from the hero entirely and rely on the showcases below to demonstrate breadth

The current placement is conversion-eroding on mobile because the CTA isn't visible without scrolling on small phones (iPhone SE, older Android).

**On Elena's "live ticker" suggestion:** strongly second this for mobile. On a 375px viewport, a horizontally-scrolling row of 3 recent-report cards directly below the hero gives the visitor *immediate proof of life* without any scroll commitment. Costs us ~120px of vertical real estate but earns it back in engagement.

## Aria — Trust & credibility (revising)

The mass-market pivot doesn't trade away credibility — it shifts where credibility appears.

In Round 1, I argued for *specific named patterns* and *concrete numbers* in the hero. With the recalibration, those specifics move:
- **Above-the-fold:** identification ("you're not the only one"). Credibility is implied by the community framing, not asserted.
- **Mid-page (DataProofCTA, FAQ):** specifics. "98,427 first-person reports. 47 source archives. 1,463 distinct phenomena." "Paradocs is a research utility, not a belief system." These reach the curious-research sub-audience that's read this far.

The trick is: **the curious-research sub-audience is patient.** They'll scroll for credibility signals. The mass-market visitor is not patient and doesn't need credibility *first* — they need *recognition* first. Then they'll pay attention to the credibility signals on screens 2-3.

What I'd specifically keep above-the-fold for the trust angle: the visual treatment must not feel like a tabloid. The dark mode + restrained color + clean typography from V11.17.1 are doing the heavy lifting here. **Avoid:** exclamation marks, sensational claims, "shocking truth" rhetoric, neon green-glow effects, anything that smells like a paranormal-conspiracy aggregator. The page can be warm AND credible — that's the Letterboxd lane.

## Synthesized Round 2 recommendations

**Primary hero (revised):**

```
Headline:  "You're not the only one who's seen it."

Subhead:   "Paradocs is where people share what they couldn't quite
            explain — sightings, encounters, dreams, missing time,
            the things you'd hesitate to mention at dinner.
            98,000+ first-person accounts and growing. Find your story."

Trust line (smaller, below CTA, reordered for mobile fold):
            "UFOs, ghosts, cryptids, NDEs, glitches, déjà vu, all the
             things people don't talk about over coffee."

Primary CTA:    "See who else has seen it →"
Secondary:      "or browse without signing up →"
```

**Add an Elena-style "live activity" element below the hero:**

A horizontally-scrolling row on mobile, a 3-column grid on desktop, showing the 3-5 most-recent approved reports (title snippet + location + time-ago). Auto-refreshes every 30s. Wired to an existing endpoint (`/api/recent-reports?limit=5` if it exists, or build one). Tells the visitor "people are sharing right here, right now."

**HowItWorks section intro (replacing "From raw data to research-ready insights in three steps"):**

```
"How Paradocs works"

(subhead) "From an experience you can't explain to a community
           of people who've had something like it."
```

**HowItWorks Step 02 (already updated, but tweak to match mass-market voice):**

> Title: *"The Index surfaces the patterns"*  → *"Paradocs surfaces the patterns"*
> Description: *"Geographic clusters, temporal correlations, and phenomenological links — the connections that are invisible when reports sit scattered across siloed databases emerge clearly when tens of thousands are organized together."*  → *"Where reports cluster — same shape, same region, same hour of the night — patterns become visible. Patterns nobody could see in any single account."*

Less archival, more cinematic. The visitor gets it instantly.

**HowItWorks Step 03 (already "You investigate" — too research-coded):**

> Title: *"You investigate"*  → *"You find your story"*
> Description: *"Search, filter, and map the full Index. Save what matters. Cross-reference cases against your own experience. Find connections nobody else has found."*  → *"Search what you saw. Save the cases that match. Add your own experience. See who else has seen something like it — in your area, your decade, your situation."*

The verbs change from "search / filter / map / cross-reference" (researcher) to "search / save / add / see who else" (community member).

**LabShowcase rewrite — different positioning:**

Currently positioned as the "deep research workspace." Reposition as **"go deeper if you want."** Headline: *"Build your own case"* (not "Investigate the unknown"). Subhead: *"For when one report isn't enough — pull together everything you've found on a phenomenon, region, or pattern. The researcher mode is here when you're ready."*

This deliberately positions the heavy tooling as *optional advanced*. The casual visitor can ignore it. The researcher discovers it.

**Section ordering — Elena's amplification:**

Make FeedShowcase the dominant showcase, not one of three equals. Larger visual treatment, more content, "this is the experience." Map + Lab become "and there's more if you want it."

**Live activity component (new):**
Place between QuickNavStrip and HowItWorks. Builds the "people are here" feeling before the explainer.
