## 1. The Lay-Person Finding Card Pattern

A Finding Card is the first thing a casual visitor reads about a pattern in the archive. She is not a researcher. She has 4 seconds and a thumb. Every sentence on the card has to clear the same bar: a 47-year-old who saw a Netflix UFO doc once should nod and screenshot, not squint and re-read.

The pattern, six rules.

**Rule 1 — Open with people, not percentages.** Subject is always "X witnesses describe…" or "Across X accounts, Y describe…" Never open with "A descriptor present in…" or "The corpus shows…" The reader needs a human in the first six words.

**Rule 2 — Name the experience in plain English.** Translate every taxonomy slug at the surface. *Perception-sensory family* becomes "sleep paralysis reports." *Consciousness-practices family* becomes "meditation accounts" (or "people deep in meditation," when the verb form reads better). *Psychological-experiences family* becomes "near-death experiences" or "out-of-body experiences" — pick the one closest to the actual descriptor. Acronyms (NDE, OBE, SP) are banned on the card. They live on the detail page if anywhere.

**Rule 3 — Always anchor with an absolute count.** A percentage alone reads as either too big or too small depending on the reader's mood. *"4%"* is a shrug. *"2,643 witnesses across roughly 92,000 cases"* is a fact. Pair them: percentage plus count, or count plus percentage. Never a bare percentage.

**Rule 4 — The surprise lands last.** The card is structured like a setup-payoff joke. The setup names the three settings (ghost reports, UFO sightings, meditation accounts). The payoff is the cross-cutting beat: *"the rate barely moves"* or *"the same percentage shows up everywhere"* or *"three settings that otherwise share nothing."* Never bury the payoff inside a noun phrase.

**Rule 5 — Under 60 words. Two sentences preferred.** Three is the ceiling. The reunion sentence Helena cleared is 53 words across two sentences. The shadow-figure sentence is 30. That's the size of the box.

**Rule 6 — No compound dependent clauses.** Every clause has to survive being screenshotted in isolation. If the meaning collapses when the reader sees only the second sentence, the structure is wrong.

What the pattern is NOT. It is not the detail-page voice. It does not cite the taxonomy. It does not say "filed as" or "the corpus" or "documented accounts" (use "reports" or "accounts" or "stories"). It does not hedge with "suggesting" or "appearing to." It states the count, names the settings, and lands the surprise.

The voice is *archive-curator-talking-at-a-dinner-party*. Not the archivist's notes; the archivist explaining to a friend why the file is interesting. Concrete, calm, surprised once.

## 5. Two-layer architecture note

**The decision: the Finding Card and the Finding Detail page are two different products with two different voices.**

The Finding Card lives on the lab index, on the homepage feed, on Today special cards, and in any share-out. It is the public-facing surface — the thing the aunt reads in 4 seconds with her thumb. Its job is to make her nod, screenshot, and either move on satisfied or tap through. The voice is plain English, concrete counts, payoff-at-the-end. No taxonomy, no acronyms, no database verbs.

The Finding Detail page at `/lab/patterns/[slug]` is the click-down surface. It is where the curious reader, the skeptic, and the researcher all converge. Its job is to give the rate breakdown by family, the methodology, the chi-square or whatever rigor we choose to expose, the linked representative reports, and the editorial-register commentary. Taxonomy terms, acronyms with first-mention definitions, and database verbs are fine here. This is the layer where "perception-sensory family" is the right phrase, because the reader has self-selected into wanting that depth.

**Current state.** The detail page is Sprint 2 work. It does not exist yet. The "See reports →" CTA on the current card still points to a representative report (a single case from the corpus), not to a detail page. That is the right MVP behavior: the card explains the pattern, the link gives the reader one concrete case so the pattern feels real. When the detail page ships in Sprint 2, the CTA on the card will swap to "See the breakdown →" or similar, and the representative report will become one click deeper.

**The implication for this rewrite.** Every database term we strip out of the card lives on the detail page when it ships. We are not deleting taxonomy; we are deferring it to the layer where it belongs.
