## 2. Why v1 flagships were rejected — founder's reasoning, translated

**Family Plan.** The founder identified a fatal coordination problem. The Paradocs buyer is the person who already has the experience and the urge to document it — typically self-motivated, often the only person in their household with that interest. "Dad" or "sister" doesn't share the obsession and won't onboard, which means the Family Plan's 2-3 extra seats sit idle, and the buyer has paid the Pro premium for capacity they cannot activate. The Ancestry comparison is real but asymmetric: Ancestry's family-tree object is *inherently shared* — your tree is your aunt's tree — so the second seat activates itself. Paradocs' Record is *inherently personal* — your triangle sighting is not your dad's triangle sighting. The seat economics break.

**Human Research Consultation.** Three founder-killers. First, it introduces human labor into a model that was explicitly designed to be 100% AI-mediated — even at 75 hrs/mo of part-timer cost, it adds an operational surface (hiring, training, QA, SLA monitoring) the company doesn't otherwise need. Second, "5 business days" is a customer-service promise; missing it once kills trust for the cohort it was meant to convert. Third, the unit economics get fragile fast at scale — a viral month doubles consultation demand and either the SLA slips or operator hours go non-linear. The founder's instinct that "this isn't our model" is correct; the v1 panel mis-weighted the emotional draw of human attention against the operational gravity it would create.

**The common error.** Both v1 flagships solved Pro's differentiation problem by *adding a different product* (a shared-tree product; a concierge-service product) on top of the documentary catalogue. The correct move is to make Pro a *deeper version of the same product* — same documentary, same archival voice, but at depths the Basic user genuinely cannot reach by waiting.

### Eliminations and why

- **Real-time refresh & Personal data visualizations** are dial-turns on Basic. Same surface, finer cadence or prettier chart. They fail the "categorically different" test on inspection. Keep them as Pro perks, not flagships.
- **Beta access** is a perk, not a flagship. Brand fit is mediocre (it imports the SaaS-early-adopter frame).
- **Custom watchlists** and **Custom phenomenology lens** are strong power-user features but they require the user to *do work* to extract value (define criteria, tune lenses). Pro flagships should pay off passively for the founder's stated audience (45-65, documentary-leaning). Keep both as Pro features; neither carries the page alone.
- **Multi-Record / private collections** is interesting and clean (no multi-user friction by construction), but it serves a niche the panel cannot size — the user who wants to keep "their experiences" separate from "stories they've collected" is real but small. It's a feature, not a flagship.
- **Catalogue annotations** is a journaling overlay; good Pro perk, weak flagship (low immediate value before the user has annotated much).
- **API access** is correctly held for a later $29 Pro+ tier (v1 already flagged this). The buyer here is a researcher / journalist segment, not the mass Pro market. Defer.
- **Rarity / probability scoring** is brand-adjacent ("your 1987 sighting matches a pattern documented in 0.3% of UFO reports") but flirts with gamification. The line between "documentary rarity" and "rare-card-pull dopamine" is thin and the brand can't afford to cross it. Keep as a *line inside* the dossier, not a flagship surface.
- **AI conversational research agent** scored 9 and was the panel's most-debated cut. It's powerful and shippable, but the chat surface is *culturally wrong* for the documentary archive — it imports a ChatGPT register the brand has spent the entire V3 voice rewrite removing. The dossier and documentary deliver the same insight in editorial voice; the chat does it in conversational voice and pulls the brand off-axis. Hold for v3 of the Pro tier; not now.
- **Time-Machine context** scored 9 and lost to the dossier on a partial-overlap argument: the contextual lens (weather, news, contemporaneous events) is one *category* of cross-reference the auto-dossier already surfaces. Folding Time-Machine into the dossier as a section ("Context of the date and place") gets 80% of the value at zero additional surface. Keep as a dossier sub-feature, not a standalone flagship.

### Winners — why

- **Cross-reference dossier** is the AI-version of the rejected Human Consultation. Same emotional payoff ("a thorough search of the Archive specifically for *your* account"), zero human labor, refreshes automatically. It's the flagship the v1 panel was reaching for and missed.
- **AI-narrated personal documentary** is what the v1 panel held back as "interesting but cost-uncertain." Costs are now tractable on Haiku (~$0.01-0.03 per 1500-word essay at current pricing; refresh quarterly or on material Archive growth = ~$0.10/user/year), and the brand-fit is *exact*. This is the museum-membership artifact.
- **Rich PDF export of the documentary book** is the supporting flagship — it ships the documentary as a downloadable, formatted, footnoted archive object. It closes the loop the v1 panel left open ("Pro feels like a different *product*"), because the user now owns a *physical-feeling* artifact at the end. PDF export is already on the Pro stack in V3; the flagship-grade move is treating it as a *book* (with cover page, TOC, citations, phen-page references), not a data export.

## 4. Final Pro flagship stack

### Flagship 1 — The Cross-Reference Dossier

**What it is.** For every experience in your Record, Pro auto-generates and continuously refreshes an exhaustive dossier that maps your account into the Archive. Sections:

- **Closest reports** — top 20 nearby in fingerprint signal, ranked, each with one-line synopsis and citation
- **Phenomenology lineage** — which catalogue phen pages your account inherits from, with explanatory paragraphs
- **Geographic neighbors** — every Archive report within a 200-mile/50-year radius, mapped and listed
- **Temporal neighbors** — reports from the same season, lunar phase, time of day, decade
- **Descriptor matches** — language-pattern siblings (same imagery, same emotional register)
- **Context of the date and place** — contemporaneous Archive activity (other reports that week within the region), and where possible, structured contextual fields available in the Archive metadata
- **Rarity reading** — one paragraph, documentary register, on how statistically distinctive the account is vs. the corpus

**Refresh.** Daily nightly job recomputes; surface notes the last refresh time. On-demand "rebuild now" button.

**UX sketch.** Renders as a tab on each experience's dossier called *The Dossier* (Pro only — Basic sees a teaser footnote: "Pro builds the full cross-reference dossier for every experience in your Record"). The dossier is long — easily 2000-3000 words of structured material per experience — and intentionally so; it is the deep archival reading the Human Consultation was *promising*, delivered without the SLA.

**Why it works.** Brand: this is what a real museum archivist would mail you a printout of. Operational: pure AI + RPC queries, zero humans. Constraint #1-5 all green.

### Flagship 2 — Your Documentary

**What it is.** Haiku-generated long-form documentary essay (800-1500 words) treating your Record as the subject. Written in the same archival, documentary-narrator voice the rest of the product uses. Not a summary; an *essay* — it situates your experiences against the corpus, surfaces what the Archive thinks they're part of, draws threads, names patterns, quotes you back to yourself when relevant.

**Refresh cadence.** Auto-refreshes when (a) you submit a new experience, (b) the Archive grows by ≥5% since last generation, or (c) on-demand via "regenerate" button. Older versions are kept and viewable — the documentary has an *edition history*.

**UX sketch.** A dedicated tab on the Record called *Your Documentary*. Renders in editorial reading-mode typography (serif, generous line-height, no chrome). Top of the essay carries a kicker — "An essay drawn from your Record and the Archive. Edition VII. Generated October 2026." — and a footer with a regenerate button and an edition log. Basic users see the *first paragraph* as a teaser and a footnote: "Pro generates the full essay and keeps the edition history."

**Why it works.** It is the flagship that *most physically embodies the brand promise* — a documentary catalogue of the self. Cost is negligible at Pro volumes. No human labor. No multi-user dependency. Categorically different from Basic's two-sentence synthesis: this is the difference between a footnote and a chapter.

### Flagship 3 (supporting) — The Documentary Book (PDF)

**What it is.** A one-click export that compiles *Your Documentary* + *The Dossier* (all experiences) + selected Record material into a formatted PDF book. Cover page with edition number, table of contents, footnotes, citations to Archive reports and phen pages, a closing index. Documentary-typography throughout — this is intentionally a book-feeling artifact, not a JSON dump.

**Refresh.** Re-export anytime; carries the edition number of the current Documentary. Pro also retains raw JSON / KML / CSV export (already in V3 matrix) for the power-user case.

**UX sketch.** "Export your Documentary Book" button at the top of the Documentary tab and on the Subscription page's active-benefits list. PDF generation runs server-side (existing stack supports this); 30-60s render; user gets a download link and an email.

**Why it works.** Closes the loop. Pro isn't "more dials"; it produces a *thing* the user can hold (print, send to family, archive). This is what makes the museum-membership frame literal. Constraint #3 (categorically different) is fully resolved by the book artifact itself.

## 5. Annual plan — comparable products audit

### The comparable landscape

| Product | Annual offered? | Discount vs. monthly | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ancestry.com** | Yes | ~25-50% (6-mo and annual SKUs) | Aggressive annual push; nearly all paid users on annual |
| **MyHeritage** | Yes | ~50% (annual is the default price; monthly is a premium) | Heavy annual default |
| **23andMe** | One-time + annual add-on (Health+) | Health+ at $69/yr, no monthly equivalent | Hybrid; annual is the only sub option |
| **Strava Premium** | Yes | ~17% ($79.99/yr vs. $11.99/mo = $143.88) | Standard 17% annual discount |
| **Letterboxd Pro / Patron** | Yes | $19/yr Pro (no monthly); $49/yr Patron (no monthly) | Annual-only; no monthly SKU |
| **NYT Digital** | Yes | ~50% (intro rates); steady-state ~20% | Aggressive intro discounting |
| **Substack (individual pubs)** | Yes | Publisher-set, typically 15-20% off monthly×12 | Industry norm |
| **Storyworth** | Annual-only ($99/yr) | n/a — no monthly SKU | Memoir/legacy product; closest brand peer |
| **Calm** | Yes | ~67% ($69.99/yr vs. $14.99/mo) | Aggressive — wellness norm |
| **Headspace** | Yes | ~58% ($69.99/yr vs. $12.99/mo) | Aggressive — wellness norm |
| **Notion Plus** | Yes | ~20% ($96/yr vs. $120 monthly) | Standard SaaS |
| **Day One Premium** | Annual-only ($34.99/yr) | n/a | Journaling product; brand peer |

### The pattern

**10 of 12 offer annual.** Of those: the wellness products (Calm, Headspace) discount most aggressively (50-67%); the SaaS/productivity products (Strava, Notion, Substack) cluster at 15-20%; the legacy/journaling/memoir products (Storyworth, Day One, Letterboxd) skip the monthly SKU entirely and ship *annual-only* at a low price.

**The two closest brand peers — Storyworth ($99/yr) and Day One ($34.99/yr) — are annual-only.** This is meaningful: products that frame themselves as long-form, archival, reflective experiences correctly recognize that monthly billing is a poor fit for a multi-year reading cadence. The user opens the product quarterly, sometimes less; monthly billing creates a friction-to-value mismatch every cycle.

### Recommendation

**Ship annual at launch. Keep both monthly and annual SKUs.** Going annual-only is the brand-purer move (and the panel discussed it seriously), but for v1 it's too aggressive — the monthly SKU is the price-shock-absorber for cold visitors who want to try Basic without a $59 commitment.

**Pricing: $59 Basic annual / $149 Pro annual.** Validates against industry norm (17% off) and matches v1 recommendation.

- $59/yr Basic = 17.9% off $71.88 monthly × 12
- $149/yr Pro = 17.2% off $179.88 monthly × 12

**Why not deeper.** Wellness-tier discounts (50%+) would price Pro annual at ~$90, which (a) under-prices the documentary book artifact, (b) signals a "discount-driven" brand that conflicts with museum-membership voice, and (c) burns margin we don't need to burn — Pro buyers are not price-elastic at this level.

**Default toggle.** Monthly default on `/pricing` (lower friction to start), with annual price shown as strikethrough adjacent ("$5.99/mo or $59/yr — save $12.88"). Per Pricing Panel §3.2/§8.2.

**One-time switch offer.** Day-30 email to monthly subscribers: "Switch to annual and save the next 12 months at 17% off." Single email, no follow-up — documentary brand, not nag brand.

## 6. Final Pro feature stack (pricing-page copy spec)

**Pro — $14.99/month or $149/year. Everything in Basic, plus:**

- **The Dossier** — for every experience in your Record, an auto-generated cross-reference dossier mapping your account into the Archive: closest reports, phenomenology lineage, geographic and temporal neighbors, descriptor matches, contextual notes, and a rarity reading. Refreshed nightly.
- **Your Documentary** — a long-form documentary essay treating your Record as the subject, written in the catalogue's archival voice. Refreshed when your Record grows or the Archive expands. Edition history kept.
- **The Documentary Book** — one-click export of your Documentary and Dossiers as a formatted PDF book with cover page, table of contents, footnotes, and citations.
- **Advanced lenses on every comparative surface** — Pro depth on temporal, geographic, and sentiment per V3 §2 (configurable date-ranges, county-level density, KML export, multi-dimensional sentiment, trajectory analysis).
- **Custom watchlists** — saved searches that notify you when matching reports land in the Archive.
- **Private annotations** on any catalogue report or phenomenon page — your commentary layer, private to you, exportable into the Documentary Book.
- **Real-time Record refresh** with on-demand regenerate.
- **Top-5 named-match on-demand** with full mutual opt-in handshake.
- **Permissioned shareable Record links** (share only the Documentary; share only one Dossier; share only the geographic surface).
- **Multi-year Year-in-Review retrospective.**
- **Raw data export** — JSON, full match list, all citations, county-level density data, KML.
- **First-look status** on freshly ingested reports that match your fingerprint.

**Held for later (deliberately not at MVP launch):**
- Multi-Record / parallel private collections (interesting but unsized audience; revisit at 12 months)
- AI conversational research agent (voice-mismatch with documentary brand; revisit when chat UX can be made archival)
- API access / Pro+ at $29 (researcher segment; ship when demand surfaces)
- Catalogue annotations as a *social* layer (currently private only)
- Custom phenomenology lens (power-user; ship as a Pro perk, not a flagship)
