## 2. The Gaia-aligned pattern (~300 words)

A Finding Card is the first thing a spiritually curious adult — 35 to 65, watches Graham Hancock on Gaia, reads *Many Lives Many Masters*, has had at least one unexplained experience herself — reads about a pattern in the archive. She is not a skeptic. She is not a believer. She is the third audience: *the one who finds patterns meaningful because they hint at something larger, but who recoils from anyone selling her certainty about what that something is.* Every sentence on her card has to land in the gap between "huh" and "I knew it."

The pattern, six rules.

**Rule 1 — Lead with the qualitative finding, not the math.** The first clause names what people experience, not how many people. *"The dead come back."* Not *"1,928 witnesses describe."* The number is the receipt; the experience is the headline.

**Rule 2 — Human-warm nouns only.** "People." "They." "The dead." "The living." Never "witnesses," "respondents," "accounts," "documented cases," "the corpus." Those words signal a database is talking. The card is a person noticing something out loud.

**Rule 3 — Name the three settings as places, not categories.** "At a deathbed." "In a haunting." "At a medium's table." Not "psychological accounts" or "ghost reports." The Gaia ear hears places; the academic ear hears taxonomies. She wants places.

**Rule 4 — Two-part closer with a meaning beat.** The last clause is the screenshot. It says the implication without making the claim. *"Three doorways. The same meeting."* The reader supplies the meaning; we supply the structure. This is the line that ends up in someone's Instagram story.

**Rule 5 — Strip the percentages from the card entirely.** No "2-3%." No "47%." No "barely moves." Those numbers live on the detail page where they belong. The card states the qualitative invariance: *the same thing shows up in three places that share nothing else.*

**Rule 6 — Under 35 words. Documentary register. No exclamation. No mysticism vocabulary.** Banned: "mysteriously," "spooky," "creepy," "miraculous," "transcend," "the universe is," "consciousness reveals," "spirit." The voice is *the curator of the archive saying the quiet finding plainly.* Not the guru. Not the lab. The archivist who has noticed something across the files and is telling you, evenly, what she has noticed.

What the pattern is NOT. It is not New Age. It is not statistical. It is not a content-marketing tease. It does not promise truth. It states the qualitative pattern in human language and lets the reader feel the weight of it. Past tense, third person, concrete nouns, two short sentences, one meaning beat at the end.

The voice in one image: *the archivist at a dinner party telling a friend what she noticed across the files this week, in the same tone you would say "the same bird showed up at three different windows."*

## 4. Dissents

Three places the panel split. Documenting the alternative copy so the founder can swap.

### 4.1 electromagnetic_disturbance — "The machinery knows before we do." (Helena flag, cleared)

Helena initially vetoed the closer as borderline guru-speak (machinery cannot "know"). Tariq, Lena, and Mariko all preferred the closer because it is the line that lands the screenshot. Helena cleared on the documentary-metaphor reading. This is the one closer on the set most at risk of a register objection from the founder. Conservative swap:

> "When the UFO appears, when the ghost arrives, when something steps out of the woods — watches stop, engines stall, electronics die. Three different events. The same dead electronics."

(28 words.) Drops "the machinery knows before we do" and reverts to the V11.18.12 closer pattern. Loses some screenshot energy; keeps the parallel triplet at the head.

### 4.2 paralysis — Mariko's alternative

Mariko slowed on the closer ("The same paralysis that opens sleep paralysis opens these too.") because the repeated "sleep paralysis" feels like a tongue-twister to the aunt. Helena and Lena defended the closer for its naming discipline — sleep paralysis is the comparison baseline and the sentence has to name it. Mariko's preferred alternative:

> "People find their bodies locked at the start of a near-death experience and at the start of a UFO encounter. Same locked body. Different door."

(25 words.) Drops the explicit sleep-paralysis comparison from the sentence body and pushes it to the headline only. Cleaner cadence; slight loss of taxonomic precision. Founder pick.

### 4.3 shadow_figure — "the edge of the room" vs. founder's "the other side"

The founder's prompt example for reunion uses "on the other side" for the closer. Lena proposed an alternative for shadow_figure that echoes that: *"Different doors. The same presence on the other side."* Helena flagged "the other side" as carrying afterlife connotations that are appropriate for reunion (where the figure literally is on the other side of death) but oversell for shadow_figure (where we have no claim about what side the figure is on). The panel kept "The same presence." as the closer for shadow_figure and reserved the "other side" phrasing for reunion only. Documented in case the founder wants the cadence-twin.
