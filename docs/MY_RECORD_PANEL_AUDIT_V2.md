# My Record V2 — Reimagining From Scratch

**To:** Chase
**From:** The V2 Panel (eight voices, convened June 2026)
**Re:** What Paradocs should give a user on their personal dashboard, taken back to first principles
**Anti-pattern:** `docs/MY_RECORD_PANEL_AUDIT.md` (v1). The v1 panel iterated on what was there. This panel was instructed not to. Founder rejected v1's three biggest moves (Compare Notes reframe, fix-the-PDF, trim-the-match-list) as too small.

The brief from the founder, restated in our words: *the catalogue of 200,000 reports has a unique gift to give every user — something only Paradocs can generate, that becomes the user's, and that, when shared with another user, unlocks new information for both. Ancestry calls a tree a tree; we need to name what we make.*

---

## 1. TL;DR

The flagship artifact is **the Sigil** — a generated, image-first, single-page personal mark that compresses a user's record into a small set of iconographic glyphs derived from the corpus's actual phenomenology vocabulary (descriptor families, signal motifs, temporal/geographic shape). It is the gift. It is screenshottable. It is the only thing Paradocs gives that no other product can. The flagship social mechanic is **Crossings** — when two users' Sigils share a non-obvious glyph (a rare descriptor, a sub-family, a temporal coincidence that the corpus tells us is statistically meaningful, not just "you both saw a triangle"), the system surfaces a Crossing event that, when both opt in, **reveals new corpus context to both parties** (e.g., "the seven other accounts where this exact pairing appears, drawn from the 200k archive — none of you knew about each other"). The DM is the *footer* of the Crossing, not the point. The brand-defining promise: **"Your account, set against two hundred thousand others, returns to you as a mark."** The tier ladder: Free earns a Sigil at first submission and sees Crossings happen around them; Basic ($5.99) unlocks accepting Crossings and the **Field Notebook** (a living, editorial-quality personal page that recomputes as the corpus grows); Pro ($14.99) unlocks the **Imprint** (a printable, archival-grade letterpress-style folio of the user's record, generated by the system, suitable to actually frame) plus rare-Crossing alerts and the Tracer (see §5). We kill the PDF, the dossier-as-document, the match list, the named-match offers rail, the discoverability toggle row, and the Hints rail as currently shaped. ~5,000 words follow.

## 2. The recommended My Record — what the user sees

### 2.1 Mobile (top to bottom)

1. **Header** — "My Record" wordmark, small Submit button, settings cog. No notification bell here (lives inside the Sigil card).
2. **The Sigil card** (full-bleed, square, ~1:1 aspect, ~viewport-width-minus-32). Tap-to-magnify. Long-press to share. Below the card, three tiny pill chips: **Read the marks** · **What changed** · **Imprint**.
3. **Crossings strip** — a thin horizontal row of three to six small mark-pairs, each pair being one of the user's glyphs aligned with a stranger's matching glyph. 4. **Field Notebook** (Basic+ — Free sees a *single-glyph teaser entry*) — a single, editorial, prose-and-image page that reads like an entry in The Atavist or the Public Domain Review, computed from the corpus around the user's record. 5. **Submitted experiences** — a quiet roll of the user's submitted accounts as small textual entries, dot-and-line style (no card chrome), edit/delete in a kebab on each row.
6. **Imprint preview** (Pro CTA on Free/Basic) — a small thumbnail of an actual letterpress-style folio plate with the words "Order an Imprint" beneath. Single tap to inspect the full Imprint, single tap to upgrade.

That is six surfaces. Approximate scroll: under 2.5 viewport-heights on mobile. v1 measured 6,400px on mobile; v2's target is ~1,800px.

### 2.2 Desktop

The Sigil card becomes a centerpiece roughly 600px square, sitting beside (not above) the Field Notebook in a two-column composition. The Crossings strip runs as a thin band beneath both. The Submitted experiences and Imprint preview live in a right-rail sidebar. There is no two-column-of-cards layout anywhere. The desktop composition is closer to a museum object label beside its catalogue text than to a SaaS dashboard.

## 3. The Flagship Artifact — The Sigil

### 3.1 What it is

The Sigil is a generated, image-first, personal mark. It is to a Paradocs user what a tartan is to a Scottish clan, a coat of arms is to a family, a natal chart is to an astrologer, a tattoo flash sheet is to a sailor — a compact, iconographic compression of identity into glyphs. The Sigil has six to nine small black glyphs arranged in a quiet near-symmetrical lattice on parchment, plus an edition mark and a name.

Each glyph maps to one of these underlying corpus-derived signals, picked by the generator from the user's submitted experiences and from how the user's report-set sits against the 200k archive:

- **A phen-family primary glyph** (the user's dominant family — UFO, apparition, cryptid, NDE, BEK, doppelganger, kundalini, missing-time, etc.) drawn from a fixed editorial set of ~40 marks. These are the keystones. Each is a real woodcut-quality black-line glyph commissioned once, not a generated image.
- **One to three descriptor glyphs** drawn from the corpus's actual descriptor vocabulary as it appears in the user's narrative (e.g., "triangle," "white-light," "static-tingling," "missing-time," "no-shadow," "ozone-smell"). The descriptor vocabulary lives in the engine; the glyph for each is editorial.
- **A temporal glyph** representing the band where the user's reports cluster against the corpus's bimodal hour-of-day curve (e.g., "between Vespers and the Hour of the Wolf" — a thin marker on a small clock-face glyph). This is the corpus's truth, not the user's claim.
- **A spatial glyph** representing the topology the user's reports sit in — coastal, ridgeline, riparian, floodplain, alpine, abandoned-industrial. Computed from a coarse terrain classification of the user's lat/long centroid against the corpus.
- **A rarity glyph** — a small ordinal mark indicating how thin the slice of corpus the user's pattern sits inside is. Not a percentile number; a glyph (one bar = common, three bars = thin, an asterisk = singular).
- **A "third-party" glyph** that ONLY appears when the corpus contains other accounts that share four or more of the user's glyphs. This is the social-discovery hook surfaced *on the artifact itself*. Eight to twenty users sharing the same third-party glyph form what we call a *cohort* (see §4).

The result is a composed mark that looks more like a witch-mark in a Reformation pamphlet, a sailor's tattoo flash, or a hobo sign than like an avatar or badge. The register is austere by design. There is no color. There is no chrome. There is one font for the edition mark (small-caps EB Garamond), one font for the user name (italic Garamond), and the glyphs themselves.

### 3.2 Why it is unique

No other product can give a user a Sigil because no other product has Paradocs's corpus to draw on. The artifact is generative *from* the corpus. The descriptor glyphs are real. The temporal glyph is a real distribution. The rarity glyph is a real percentile. The third-party glyph would not exist for a user whose pattern sits alone — and the user knows that, because they would not see one.

Compare to Spotify Wrapped: Spotify can give you "your top artists" because they have the listening data. Paradocs can give you a Sigil because we have the phenomenology data. Compare to a natal chart: an astrologer compresses your birth time into a mark. We compress your record into a mark, and our mark has the additional property of being *verifiable*. Compare to a Letterboxd "Four Favorites": same idea (compress identity to a small grid), but with corpus-grounded glyphs that have meaning beyond preference.

### 3.4 How it's generated

The generator is a deterministic function over signals the engine already computes:

- Phen-family aggregation per user (already in `experiences` join)
- Descriptor extraction per report (already in the Sonnet 4.6 narrative pass; raw descriptors land in `paradocs_narrative.descriptors`)
- Hour and decade distribution against corpus (already in `/api/lab/temporal-distribution`)
- Terrain classification (new — small server-side lookup over MapTiler's terrain tiles, ~50 LOC, cached per H3 cell)
- Rarity percentile (already computed in `ProDossier.rarity_percentile`)
- Cohort lookup (new — find users whose Sigil shares ≥4 glyphs; this is the same Postgres function family as the existing `match_score` constellation RPC, with the glyph set replacing the signal set)

The composition is rule-based, not ML. The lattice is fixed (a 5×5 grid with 6-9 slots filled). Glyphs are picked by priority: family glyph(s) always central; descriptor glyphs flanking; temporal and spatial in the corners; rarity centered low; third-party glyph in the lower-center *only when earned*. A user's Sigil is **deterministic from their record state** — same record → same Sigil — which means edition numbers tick when the record meaningfully changes, and an old Sigil is a real historical artifact, not a stale render.

### 3.5 Share mechanics

The Sigil image is the share unit. The system generates three formats from one SVG source:

- **Square** (1080×1080) for social feeds. Glyphs only, edition mark, user name. No URL, no Paradocs wordmark, just a small "paradocs.io/sigil/<short-id>" hand-typeset at the bottom edge.
- **Story** (1080×1920) for vertical feeds. Sigil centered with parchment extending top and bottom, a single line of catalogue text above ("Read at paradocs.io/sigil/<short-id>") and the edition mark below.
- **Print plate** (300dpi A6) for users who want to actually print the thing. This is the bridge to the Pro Imprint.

The shareable URL `paradocs.io/sigil/<short-id>` resolves to a public-facing page that renders the Sigil, the user's chosen name, and a single editorial paragraph explaining the glyphs ("This Sigil belongs to a contributor who has logged a triangle UFO event from a ridgeline location between the hours of midnight and 4am. The * glyph signals that the corpus contains no exactly-matching prior account."). Anyone who reaches that page can be prompted "Find what your Sigil would be" with a single-click submission flow. This is the acquisition loop.

### 3.6 Why this is gift-worthy

A friend can screenshot it. A user wants to caption it. It is small enough to use as a profile picture. It rewards a second look (every glyph is decipherable from a legend). It feels like it was *made for them* because it was. It does not feel like a Paradocs marketing card.

The editorial lead's stamp: **ON-BRAND.** "The Sigil is the documentary register made into an object. No color drift, no spookiness, no avatar-creator gameification. The register is closer to a Reformation pamphlet than to a video-game class badge. The single editorial risk is the glyph set itself becoming twee; the panel commits to commissioning glyphs from a real woodcut artist or in that idiom, not from generic Lucide icons."

## 4. The Flagship Social Mechanic — Crossings

### 4.1 What it is

A Crossing is an event the system fires when two users' Sigils share, by coincidence, a configuration of glyphs that the corpus says is meaningfully rare. The trigger is not "you both submitted a UFO sighting." The trigger is "you both submitted a triangle UFO event seen from a ridgeline between midnight and 4am with a no-shadow descriptor — and the corpus contains only seven such accounts total." The rarity is the gate. The system computes Crossings nightly, against the same edges the constellation RPC already computes for the existing match list, but with the additional constraint that the matched signal-set sits in the long tail of the corpus distribution.

### 4.2 Trigger and parties' actions

When a Crossing fires for the first time on User A, the Crossings strip on A's My Record gains a small mark-pair (their Sigil glyphs aligned with the stranger's matching subset). A subtle marker tells A "this is new." A taps the mark-pair → opens a Crossing card. The card shows:

- The matching glyph subset (visually, mark-on-mark)
- A single editorial sentence ("You and another contributor share an unusually thin slice of the archive.")
- Two CTAs: **"Accept the Crossing"** and **"Not now"**

User B sees the same on their end. Neither party knows who the other is, and the card carries no identity hints — no avatar, no name, no general location, no submission date.

When both A and B accept, the system reveals — to both — what was previously private. The revelation is the gift.

### 4.3 What's revealed

This is the Ancestry-tree-merge moment. **The revelation has three components, and none of them is "you can now DM."**

1. **The cohort.** Both users learn how many other accounts in the 200k corpus share the same thin signal-set — and they see those accounts as small unattributed entries in a single editorial paragraph ("Seven other accounts in the Paradocs Archive describe an event of this exact shape. They were logged in 1973, 1988, 1991, 1997, 2003, 2014, and 2020. The 1973 account was filed by an off-duty radar operator. The 1988 account is the only one with a corroborating photograph. The 2014 account is the only one to mention a smell."). Neither user could have synthesized this paragraph from their own record; the catalogue did it for them. **This is the new information.**
2. **Each other's submission excerpt.** Not the full report — a single curated excerpt the other party chose to surface (the user picks the excerpt at acceptance time from their own record). This avoids the "verbatim side-by-side" pattern the founder rejected. The user is sharing a *quote*, like an epigraph.
3. **A private joint surface — the Joint Page.** The cohort paragraph, the two excerpts, and the matched glyph subset live forever on a single private page accessible only to the two parties. The page is dated, citable, and exportable as a single image (the V2 of share mechanics). The Joint Page is the durable artifact of the Crossing.

Below the Joint Page, optionally, a small composer for a single message — labeled *"Send one note"* rather than "Open a chat." DMs do not exist as a separate inbox. There is no thread list. There is no chat affordance until *after* a Crossing has been accepted, and even then it is one composer, one optional note, no thread. If both parties want to keep talking, they exchange real contact details inside the note. **The product is not a chat product.**

### 4.4 Permanent vs ephemeral

- **Permanent on the user's record:** the Crossing entry in their strip, the Joint Page, the cohort paragraph.
- **Ephemeral:** the Crossing card before acceptance (auto-dismisses after 30 days), the discoverability state (per-experience, default off, the user controls it from a glyph-context-menu on the Sigil, not from a row).
- **Never public:** the other user's identity, location, or full report.

### 4.5 Why this beats the v1 "Compare Notes" reframe

v1's Compare Notes was a side-by-side reading view. Two reports next to each other. Founder said this adds little. The Crossings mechanic adds the third thing: **the catalogue's testimony**. The corpus *is the third party in the room*. Two users alone is a conversation; two users plus the corpus's own seven-account paragraph is a *finding*. Neither user authored the finding; both unlocked it.

Compare to Pokémon Go's friend mechanic, which trades a tangible object (a Pokémon) when two friends connect — that object is genuinely new to both. Compare to Spotify Blend, which generates a playlist *from* the intersection of two users — that playlist is new to both. Crossings work in the same way: the cohort paragraph is generated *from* the intersection of two records plus the corpus, and is genuinely new information.

The editorial lead's stamp: **ON-BRAND, with one flag.** "The cohort paragraph is the load-bearing element. If we generate it with too much Sonnet flourish, it becomes spooky-tour-guide voice. We commit to a deterministic template seeded with corpus facts only — dates, counts, the single most distinguishing detail per account — and an editorial review pass before any Crossing card is exposed to a real user. Tone is closer to a museum wall card than to a podcast cold-open."

## 5. Secondary artifact + mechanic candidates

### 5.1 The Field Notebook (secondary artifact)

A weekly-recomputed editorial-prose page in the user's record, written by a small templated generator over corpus signals adjacent to the user's pattern. It reads like one entry of a research diary — not "your dashboard." Four to seven short paragraphs, one map plate, one image plate, one footnote rule. The Notebook surfaces things the catalogue noticed that the user did not ask for: *"A second triangle UFO account was logged this week from a ridgeline within 50 miles of yours. It was filed by a contributor who was 14 years old at the time of the event in 1991. The Notebook will follow this thread."*

The Notebook is secondary because it is consumption-shaped, not gift-shaped — it is something the user *reads*, not something the user *holds*. It deepens retention without serving acquisition. The flagship Sigil pairs with the Notebook the way a museum object label pairs with the catalogue essay — one is the image, one is the prose. Both belong on the same page. What would make it flagship in a future version: if we figured out how to make the Notebook itself shareable (a serialized public Notebook that strangers subscribe to — the Substack pattern).

### 5.2 The Imprint (secondary artifact, Pro flagship)

A physical, mailable, letterpress-style folio of the user's record. Single A5 plate, deckle-edge, printed on heavy stock with the user's Sigil as the recto and a hand-set catalogue card of their record on the verso. Generated as a print-ready PDF by our system, but the Pro tier optionally has us print and mail it (single one-off, then optionally one per major edition). Cost to Paradocs at <$8 per Imprint at MOQ 100 with a small-batch letterpress partner; pricing point lets us bundle two per year into Pro at $14.99/mo without margin damage. This *replaces* the broken PDF dossier in spirit and in function — it is what the founder probably wanted when they originally said "PDF book."

What would make it flagship: if every Pro user received one as part of onboarding (we'd need to confirm unit economics at scale, but the framing — "Pro members receive a printed Imprint of their record" — is genuinely category-defining for a paranormal product).

### 5.3 The Tracer (secondary mechanic, Pro)

A "leave-a-trace" mechanic — a Pro user can mark a single descriptor or signal in their Sigil with a tracer. When a *future* submission (not just an existing one) lands in the corpus matching that tracer, the user is alerted with a small Crossing-shaped event. This is the watchlist V2: pointed at the *shape* of an experience rather than at a geographic-temporal box. Mechanically simpler than the current Watchlists rail because it lives inside the Sigil object rather than as a separate surface.

### 5.4 The Quiet Register (secondary mechanic — research, not ship-yet)

For users with extraordinarily thin Sigils (rarity = singular on the third-party glyph), the system can offer to introduce the user to a corpus curator (a human, Paradocs's editorial team) for a single private exchange. This is rare-by-design — fires perhaps a dozen times a year — and serves both as the highest-value moment in the product and as a quality signal back into the corpus. Not a flagship because it requires human labor; flagged as a research thread for V3.

## 7. Tier value redistribution

### 7.1 Free — "you get your mark"

**Flagship:** the Sigil. Generated at first submission. Updates on subsequent submissions. Shareable, screenshottable, owned by the user. The Free Sigil is the *full* Sigil — we do not cripple it. Users without third-party-glyph rarity get a Sigil without the third-party glyph, which is honest, not a downgrade.

**Supports:**
- See the Crossings strip with strangers' mark-pairs visible (cannot accept them).
- See the locked Field Notebook (first paragraph rendered; rest masked with a single upgrade CTA).
- See the Imprint preview thumbnail with the words "Pro members receive a printed Imprint."

### 7.2 Basic — "you get the Crossings"

**Flagship:** Crossings — accept them, unlock cohort paragraphs, build Joint Pages. This is the social mechanic in full. $5.99 buys you the ability to meet the corpus through other contributors and have the corpus testify back. This is what the founder's vision asks for.

**Supports:**
- Field Notebook in full, weekly recomputed.
- Sigil edition history (every prior Sigil archived as a small dated thumbnail strip below the current one — a record of how the mark has evolved).
- Unlimited submissions of experience (already true on Free; preserved here).

### 7.3 Pro — "you get the object"

**Flagship:** the Imprint — a printed, mailed, archival-grade letterpress folio of the user's record. Two per year included. Pro membership becomes a *physical-object subscription* on top of the digital tier, which is rare in mass-market consumer SaaS and a real moat. Users will literally show this artifact to their friends.

**Supports:**
- The Tracer mechanic — leave a trace on a descriptor; future matching submissions ping you.
- Rare-Crossing alerts — when a Crossing fires with a singular rarity (the third-party-glyph cohort has fewer than five members in the entire corpus), Pro users hear about it within 24 hours, not on next visit.
- The Imprint Editor — a small surface for choosing the catalogue text on the verso, choosing the user-facing name, and selecting between three editorially-curated plate compositions.

The conversion expert's note: "Free→Basic conversion is driven by the Crossings strip — users *see* mark-pairs they can't act on, and the friction is conversion-shaped, not annoying-shaped. Basic→Pro is driven by the Imprint as an aspirational physical object, which has a category-defining novelty advantage in consumer SaaS."

The mass-market designer's note: "Free is generous in a way the median curious user will understand without explanation — they get a real mark, real shareable image, real ownership. Basic and Pro asks are framed as 'unlock the rest of the catalogue's testimony' (Basic) and 'receive the printed artifact' (Pro), both of which are concrete and pictureable."

The editorial stamp on the tier ladder: **ON-BRAND.** "Free as 'the mark you earn,' Basic as 'the catalogue's testimony,' Pro as 'the printed object' — each tier is named in documentary register. No 'Enthusiast' or 'Researcher' tier-name marketing. The brand-defining promise reads coherently across all three."

## 9. Dissents and open questions for the founder

### 9.1 Dissent — the Imprint as a Pro flagship

**Conversion expert:** "Physical fulfillment at $14.99/mo is an operational lift Paradocs does not currently carry. The unit economics work at <$8/print at MOQ 100, but the operational burden — letterpress partner relationship, addressing, mailing, returns, international shipping — is real. If the founder is not ready to take on a shipping operation, the Imprint becomes a *generated-PDF-of-the-Imprint* (no print, no mail) and the Pro flagship narrative weakens significantly. The panel's recommendation stands as 'real Imprint,' but the operational risk is real and the founder needs to make this call before V2 build."

**Mass-market designer (reply):** "Even a single Imprint per Pro user per year, mailed once at signup, would land as category-defining. We do not need monthly mailings — annual is enough. Start with annual and expand."

### 9.2 Dissent — the Sigil glyph set as editorial debt

**Editorial lead:** "Commissioning ~40 phen-family keystone glyphs + ~80 descriptor glyphs + ~12 temporal + ~10 spatial + ~5 rarity + ~1 third-party glyph means ~150 glyphs total, each commissioned from a woodcut-quality artist in a consistent hand. This is real editorial work and budget. Estimated 6-10 weeks of artist time at $80-150 per glyph = $12k-22k. If this work is not commissioned to standard, the Sigil degrades to a Lucide-icon collage and the entire artifact collapses. **The Sigil's editorial integrity is its load-bearing element.** The founder should treat this as a hard prerequisite for ship, not a polish pass."

**Cultural anthropologist (reply):** "Worth noting — every paranormal social product that has failed (TruthFinder, Encounters Live, The Black Vault forum, several others) has failed because the visual register slid into kitsch. The Sigil glyph set is the place where Paradocs either earns its register or loses it. The editorial lead's flag is the most important one in this memo."

### 9.3 Dissent — Crossings cadence and supply

**Retention expert:** "Crossings rely on the user's record being rich enough to *generate* a Crossing. A user with n=1 and a common phen-family (say, a single triangle UFO) may never hear a Crossing fire because the corpus has 5,000+ triangle UFO accounts. The mechanic risks being silent for the median user. Either (a) we surface 'near-Crossings' for thinner cases (mark-pairs that share three glyphs, not four+, with a softer cohort paragraph), or (b) we accept that Crossings are rare-by-design and let the Field Notebook carry retention for those users. Panel split 5-3 toward (b) — the rarity is the point. Founder taste call."

### 9.4 Open questions

1. **The Imprint physical-fulfillment decision (above).**
2. **Editorial glyph-set commission budget and timing (above).**
3. **User naming on the Sigil.** Do users pick a pseudonym, or do we use first name + last initial? Pseudonym fits the documentary register better; first-name-last-initial fits the Ancestry pattern better. Founder call.
4. **Cohort paragraph generation — deterministic template or Sonnet?** Editorial lead recommends deterministic-only for V1. Tone risk is real if we use Sonnet.
5. **What happens to existing Pro subscribers' PDF dossiers?** We owe them notice and a migration path. Recommendation: their final PDF dossier remains accessible for 90 days, and they receive an Imprint at no extra cost as part of the migration.
