# V11.17.4 — SME Panel Review (Round 4): Brand-confidence pivot

## What changed since Round 3

Round 3 nailed the inclusion threshold ("you saw / felt / just know — you're not the only one"). Right tone. Right warmth. But the user's response after seeing it live:

> "Needs to read more mainstream, simpler, few words to say the same thing... and we are the largest database of this in the world... need the panel to optimize — think of us as a massive brand... the one and only... convince others of it."

The recalibration is clear: Round 3 reads as a *small product being kind to its visitors*. The user wants the page to read as *the category-defining brand inviting the world in*. Different posture. Same warmth, much bigger confidence.

Reference brands that pull this off:
- **Spotify:** "Music for everyone." — 3 words, claims everyone, claims category.
- **Pinterest:** "Find your inspiration." — 3 words, claims user benefit, claims category.
- **Strava:** "The #1 app for runners and cyclists." — 8 words, claims #1 explicitly, claims category.
- **Letterboxd:** "Track your films, share your tastes, and follow people you trust." — 11 words, claims user value, claims category.

Key pattern: **few words, definite article ("THE place"), category claim, no hedge.** Round 3 was 12 words across 4 sentences with no category claim. We're going to compress.

## Tomas — Category-defining brand positioning (new)

I'll start, because the user is right that this is the missing piece.

Round 3's hero is *kind*. It says "you belong here." That's necessary but not sufficient. **The brands the user listed as references (Spotify, Pinterest, Strava, Letterboxd) all also implicitly say: "and there is no other place." That implicit claim is what makes them feel mainstream.**

You build that claim through three signals:

**Signal 1: The category-defining noun.** Pinterest isn't "a place to find images." It's "your inspiration." Strava isn't "a fitness app." It's "where athletes track activities." Paradocs needs a phrase like that. *"The home of the unexplained."* *"Where strange becomes shared."* *"The unexplained, indexed."* Pick one and commit to it across the page.

**Signal 2: An explicit scale claim.** Spotify says *"100 million songs"* on the homepage. Strava says *"over 100 million athletes."* Both are honest, both are massive, both reduce the visitor's "is this a serious product?" question to nothing. Paradocs has 98,000+ first-person accounts across 47 sources. That's a number worth saying. Phrase: *"The world's largest archive of first-person paranormal accounts."* Then immediately reinforce with the number.

**Signal 3: Confidence in tone.** Round 3 said *"the things you'd hesitate to mention at dinner"* — warm but apologetic. *Hesitate* signals shame. World-class brands don't apologize for the user's behavior; they normalize it. *"The strange, the seen, the felt — finally collected."* Removes the shame, asserts the category.

**My pick for the hero, single canonical:**

```
Headline:  The home of the unexplained.

Subhead:   The world's largest archive of first-person paranormal accounts —
            sightings, feelings, dreams, coincidences. 98,000+ stories and growing.
            See who else has seen what you saw. Or share yours.
```

12 words headline + subhead. The headline is brand-confident and category-defining. The subhead immediately backs it with the scale claim ("world's largest," "98,000+"). The third sentence — *"See who else has seen what you saw"* — carries the Round 3 inclusion warmth without three lines of poetry. The "Or share yours" is the lowest-friction invitation.

This is what mainstream brand-defining homepages look like in 2026.

## Devi — Brand strategist (revising again)

Tomas is right. I've been pushing toward research-utility (Round 1) then community-warmth (Rounds 2/3) and missing the **brand-confidence** layer that makes mainstream products work. Spotify is warm AND category-defining. They are not mutually exclusive.

The "home of the unexplained" line does work I haven't gotten from any previous variant:
- *"Home"* is warm. It says you belong here.
- *"The"* is brand-confident. It says there is only one.
- *"Unexplained"* is the category — captures everything from UFOs to déjà vu without using the gated word "paranormal."

I retract my Round 2 push for "the Index" as the dominant noun-phrase. Use *"home of the unexplained"* in the hero, **and** keep "the Index" as the deeper-product noun for the actual archive feature ("Search the Index" as a CTA, "the Index contains 98,000 reports" as a fact). Two phrases doing two jobs.

**Brand voice rule for Round 4:** never use words that signal hesitation about the subject matter (*hesitate*, *taboo*, *can't quite*). The brand is confident; the visitor doesn't need to feel embarrassed about why they're here. Replace those with words that signal arrival (*finally*, *all*, *one place*, *home*).

## Jordan — Copy (Round 4)

Tomas's variant works. I'll propose two more so we have an A/B/C to pick from:

| Variant | Headline | Subhead |
|---|---|---|
| **A (Tomas)** | **The home of the unexplained.** | The world's largest archive of first-person paranormal accounts — sightings, feelings, dreams, coincidences. 98,000+ stories and growing. See who else has seen what you saw. Or share yours. |

| **C** | **Where the unexplained lives.** | The world's largest archive of first-person paranormal accounts. UFOs to déjà vu, encounters to coincidences. 98,000+ stories. Add yours and see who matches. |

My pick: **A**. *"Home"* outperforms *"place"* and *"lives"* in B/C — it's warmer and reads as the brand claiming territory, not just locating it. The subhead is the strongest of the three: it claims the category ("world's largest archive"), names the breadth, drops the number, and ends with the lowest-friction invitation.

**For "You're not the only one":** It's a great line. Don't put it in the hero in Round 4 (the new hero already does that work via "home"). Keep it as a recurring tagline through the page — maybe as a small line in the Live Activity Ticker section ("48 people sharing what they've seen right now — you're not the only one"). Or as the headline of one of the InlineSignupCTAs.

**Search button copy:** Current is just "Search." Mainstream brands rarely use bare verbs on buttons. *"See what's there →"* feels more inviting and matches the brand voice.

**Primary CTA below search:** Round 3 changed it to *"See what others have shared →"* — that still works in Round 4 but a tighter version reads better: **"See what others have seen →"** (parallel construction with the page's seen/felt/just-know thread).

## Riku — Growth marketing

Tomas + Jordan's hero shift is correct. Three things I'd add or revise:

**1. The numerical claim has to be in the FIRST sentence the visitor reads.** Currently Round 3's subhead doesn't surface the scale until a few paragraphs in. Tomas's "world's largest archive of first-person paranormal accounts" is the right phrasing AND the right position. Visitors decide "is this a real product?" in <3 seconds. The scale claim must arrive in that window.

**2. The trust line below the CTAs should disappear in Round 4.** Round 3 used it to demonstrate breadth, but the new subhead already does that (*"sightings, feelings, dreams, coincidences"*). Two lines doing the same job is one too many. Remove the trust line. Tighter page, faster scan.

**3. The Live Activity Ticker should move higher.** Currently between QuickNav and HowItWorks. Move it directly below the hero, before QuickNav even. The visitor's *first scroll* should land on real activity, not a sub-nav.

## Sora — Threshold of entry (Round 4 revision)

The brand-confidence pivot doesn't reduce inclusion. *"Home of the unexplained"* still welcomes the visitor who has only had one small strange thing — *unexplained* covers the full spectrum, déjà vu to UFOs.

But two adjustments:

Because (a) it's preceded by the warmer *"home of the unexplained"* in the hero, (b) it's in the subhead, not the headline, and (c) it carries necessary SEO weight and tells the curious-research segment we respect their vocabulary. Layered framing works: warm hook + accurate description.

**2. Make sure the search placeholder list (V11.17.3 already broadened it) stays diverse.** The 12-item list mixes dramatic (triangle UFOs) and subtle (the day the dog wouldn't go upstairs). Keep that. The placeholder is where the inclusion work happens at micro scale.
