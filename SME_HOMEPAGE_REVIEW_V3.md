## Sora — Threshold of entry (new)

This is the single most-overlooked problem in identity-formation products: **the implicit gate**. Even when you say "everyone is welcome," your headline might secretly require the visitor to be a *kind of person* before they feel included.

Round 2's *"You're not the only one who's seen it"* has a hidden gate: *seen*. The visitor mentally checks — *did I see anything?* — and a lot of people answer no. They've **felt** something, **dreamed** something, **sensed** something. They've had a moment where the rules of normal life didn't quite apply. But they didn't *see* anything paranormal in a Hollywood sense, so the headline tells them this place isn't for them.

The fix is to broaden the recognition verb. The strongest pattern I've seen in identity-formation onboarding is the **list-as-inclusion** pattern — give the visitor several different ways to recognize themselves in the first sentence, and let them pick the one that fits.

**Apply to Paradocs:**
- *"You saw something. Or you felt something. Or you can't quite explain something that happened once."*
- *"Maybe you saw it. Maybe you only felt it. Maybe you've just always wondered."*
- *"Whether you've seen it, felt it, dreamed it, or just can't shake the feeling that something happened — you're not alone."*

The list does the inclusion work. The visitor scans the items and lands on the one that matches them. The moment of recognition is the moment they decide to engage.

**Other thresholds to lower:**

1. **The "story" framing assumes a coherent narrative.** Some people have a "story" — "I was driving home from work and saw lights move impossibly." Others just have a *moment* — "I once felt watched in a hotel room and couldn't sleep." The product should welcome both. Use "moment" alongside "story." *"Share your moment. Or just browse what others have shared."*

2. **"First-person paranormal accounts" is a category gate.** "Paranormal" is a strong word — it makes the visitor decide whether they're the kind of person who'd label their thing paranormal. Many won't. Compare:
   - *"first-person paranormal accounts"* — gated by accepting the paranormal label
   - *"things you couldn't quite explain"* — gateless. Anyone fits.

3. **The "Create free account" CTA is a commitment gate before the visitor has been welcomed.** Move the CTA further down the page. Let them browse first. Conversion from a warm browse beats conversion from a cold ask — even on identity-formation products with high intent.

## Devi — Brand strategist (further revising)

Sora's reframe extends what I argued in Round 2. The brand isn't "where people who saw paranormal things gather." It's "where moments that don't quite fit get written down."

That's a different product — and it's actually closer to what Paradocs IS. The classifier processes reports across ufos_aliens, ghosts_hauntings, **psychological_experiences**, **perception_sensory**, **consciousness_practices**, religion_mythology, cryptids, esoteric_practices, psychic_phenomena. **Five of those nine are subtle/personal categories — déjà vu, sleep paralysis, near-death, premonitions, that-time-the-room-felt-wrong.** They're already in the database. The hero just doesn't reflect them.

If I'm rewriting the brand line — and I am — it'd be something like: *"Paradocs is where the moments that don't quite fit get written down — yours, and 98,000 other people's."*

Note what I dropped: *"paranormal."* The visitor who'd resist that label can still recognize themselves. The visitor who embraces it isn't excluded either — *"the moments that don't quite fit"* covers UFO sightings perfectly well.

The word "paranormal" still belongs on the page — it's good SEO, and it tells the curious-research segment the brand respects their category. Just not in the headline. Put it in the description text, the FAQ, the OG metadata.

## Riku — Growth marketing

Sora's call about the CTA gate is critical. Right now the page has a "Create free account" button in the hero — that's a commitment ask 5 seconds after landing. Mass-market identity-formation products test "no signup wall" landing pages against "signup-prompted hero" pages and the no-wall versions consistently win on both conversion and day-1 retention.

**My revised CTA strategy:**

- **Hero CTA → exploration, not commitment.** *"See what others have shared →"* (links to /discover directly). No signup ask in the hero.
- **First signup ask appears AFTER the user has browsed at least 1-2 sections** — embed it in the FeedShowcase area or as the post-feed-preview CTA. The visitor has now seen value; the ask is warm.
- **Footer CTA is the strong-ask** — visitors who scrolled this far are committing or leaving anyway.

This effectively turns the homepage into a *trailer for the Today feed*, exactly as Elena described in Round 2. The conversion mechanic shifts from "land + sign up" to "land + browse + sign up." Slower-funnel, higher-quality conversions, much higher day-1 retention.

**Lifecycle: the post-signup screen needs to be ready for this.** When someone lands on /start (signup), the page needs to greet them in the same voice. *"Last step — and you'll start seeing what others have shared in your area."* Not "Create your research account."

## Maya — Mobile UX

Three Round 3 specifics:

1. **The headline at variant A length is ~16 words across 3 sentences.** On a 375px viewport this wraps to ~5-6 lines. That's a long hero — risk: visitor doesn't read the whole headline before deciding whether to scroll.

Two fixes:
- **Display each sentence on its own line in the hero**, with deliberate line-breaks (`<br>` or `<span className="block">`):
  ```
  You saw something.
  Or you felt something.
  Or you just know.
  ```
  Reads as poetry, not as a paragraph. The visitor's eye lands on each line, doesn't get fatigued.
- Make the headline slightly smaller on mobile so the three lines fit in the same vertical space the previous shorter headline used. *3xl on mobile, 5xl on tablet, 6xl on desktop*.

2. **Live-activity ticker on mobile** should be a single horizontally-scrolling row with snap-x behavior, not a 3-column grid. Each card ~80% viewport width so the next card is visible as a hint to swipe. Three cards visible across one swipe = good. Five hidden behind it = good.

3. **No signup CTA in the hero per Riku's call.** Move the primary CTA to *"See what others have shared →"* (a /discover link, not /start). The first signup ask drops to after the Feed showcase.

## Aria — Trust (Round 3 revised)

Lowering the threshold of entry doesn't compromise credibility. It expands the credibility surface to the full nine categories Paradocs already documents (including the subtle/personal ones). The visitor who lands with a déjà-vu story is just as valid as the one with a UFO story.

The credibility risk is on the *flip side* of this expansion: if the page reads as "every weird feeling counts," the curious-research segment might decode it as "this is for credulous people." We want them to also see the credibility signal.

The fix is **layered credibility**:
- Hero: warm, inclusive, story-coded
- Mid-page DataProof block: hard numbers (98,427 reports, 47 source archives, 1,463 distinct phenomena, NUFORC/MUFON/BFRO as named sources)
- FAQ: the "research utility, not a belief system" stays — softened per Round 2 but the core claim remains. *"We document without taking a position on whether phenomena are real. We surface patterns and let you draw your own conclusions."*

Curious-research scans the page and sees both signals. Mass-market scans the page and sees recognition. Both walk away satisfied.
